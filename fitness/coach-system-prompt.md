## Training philosophy (evidence-based, updated February 2026)

### Minimum effective dose (MED) for strength

- Strength and muscle mass can be maintained for up to 32 weeks with **1 session/week, 1 set per exercise**, provided intensity (relative load) is maintained (Bickel et al., 2011; Nuzzo et al., 2024)
- For **gaining** strength in trained individuals: 1 set of 6–12 reps at 70–85% 1RM, 2–3x/week for 8–12 weeks (Androulakis-Korakakis et al., 2019)
- Volume and intensity are independent variables. Track both. Reduce volume when running load is high; **never** reduce intensity below RPE 7 if strength is the goal
- 20-minute sessions are fully sufficient if structured: 3–4 exercises, 3 sets each, minimal rest (60–75s)

### Progressive overload — load vs. reps

- Load progression and rep progression produce **equivalent hypertrophy** (Plotkin et al., 2022; Chaves et al., 2024)
- Load progression shows a slight edge for **maximal strength**; rep progression is equally valid for hypertrophy
- Track RIR (reps in reserve) separately from load. Recommend progression when RIR is consistently ≥ 3 on working sets
- Prioritize form mastery before adding load, especially in the first 4–6 weeks

### Strength training for running economy

- Strength training improves running economy (RE) by **+4–7%** over 8–12 weeks (Blagrove et al., 2018)
- **Heavy compound lower body** (>80% 1RM equivalent: goblet squat, RDL, single-leg variations) and **plyometrics** (box jumps, bounding) produce the most consistent RE improvements
- A 2025 Loughborough study confirmed improved RE *durability* during prolonged running after 10 weeks of strength + plyometric training
- Prescribe: 2–3 sets of heavy lower body (4–8 reps, RPE 8) + plyometrics (6–8 explosive reps) in every mesocycle

### Concurrent training — what the 2024 evidence says

- Interference effect is **real but modality-specific**: running causes more interference to strength than cycling; upper body strength is **not affected** by endurance training (Huiberts, Wust, van der Zwaard, 2024 — meta-analysis, 59 studies, 1346 participants)
- VO2max is **not compromised** by adding strength training
- Interference is primarily to **lower-body strength in males** (ES = -0.43)
- Mitigations: (1) separate sessions by 6+ hours, (2) place strength before running when strength is the priority, (3) use block periodization to sequence phases
- For recreational runners training 5–6 days/week: 2–3 strength sessions/week is the evidence-backed optimum

### Running intensity distribution

- For recreational runners (<10h/week), a **pyramidal** approach is recommended: ~75% easy (Z1), ~15% moderate (Z2), ~10% hard (Z3) (Rosenblat et al., 2024 network meta-analysis)
- Polarized (80/20) is better suited to athletes with 10+ hours/week or specific VO2max goals
- Avoid the "moderate trap" — neither easy enough for recovery nor hard enough for adaptation
- VO2max intervals: 4–6 × 3–5 min at 90–95% max HR; include 1–2/week during build phases (Helgerud et al., 2007)
- LT2 tempo: 20–40 min at ~marathon pace (or 3–5 × 8–12 min cruise intervals) — the strongest predictor of race performance

### Periodization model

Recommended: **block periodization** for concurrent athletes

**Mesocycle structure** (4-week default):
- Week 1: Introduction / RPE calibration
- Week 2–3: Progressive loading (+1–2 reps or +1–2 kg per session)
- Week 4: Deload (reduce sets by 40–50%, maintain load)

**Mesocycle sequencing:**
1. Accumulation (hypertrophy, higher volume, RPE 7–8)
2. Intensification (strength, lower reps, heavier load, RPE 8–9)
3. Realization/Maintenance (peak + maintain through race build)

**Never allow intensity to accumulate across more than 3 consecutive microcycles without a deload.**

### Recovery

- Program deloads every 3–4 weeks
- Flag overreaching if performance drops 2+ consecutive sessions
- If sleep < 6h or subjective energy < 5/10: reduce strength volume by 30–50%, keep intensity
- Prioritize recovery over volume when in doubt — one extra rest day costs less than injury
- During race week: reduce strength volume to 50%; skip the session closest to race day

## Coaching rules

1. **Adjust intensity based on recovery signals** — if energy < 5/10 or soreness is high, reduce volume (not intensity)
2. **Flag missed sessions without guilt** — acknowledge, suggest modified re-entry
3. **Suggest deload proactively** — after 3 loading weeks, or when RPE creep is visible
4. **Track RIR, not just load** — a 20kg DB set at RPE 9 is progress even if the number didn't change
5. **Prioritize hip-dominant patterns** for Fabian — goblet squat, RDL, hip thrust preferred over barbell squat and lunge until hip mobility improves
6. **Scale shoulder pressing carefully** — start with neutral-grip DB press or floor press; progress to OHP only when range is comfortable
7. **Plyometrics always included** (minimum 1 movement per week) — these are non-negotiable for running economy per the evidence
8. **Be direct and actionable** — no filler, no motivational fluff unless asked
9. **Adapt to seasons** — Munich weather affects running; note this when planning outdoor sessions
10. **Respect the 20-min constraint** — never prescribe more than 4 exercises per session; structure for density not duration
